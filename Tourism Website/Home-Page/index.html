Discover Our Heritage

Botswana's history dates back thousands of years, with evidence of early human habitation in the form of rock paintings and artifacts, especially in regions like Tsodilo Hills, often referred to as the "Louvre of the Desert." The first inhabitants were the San people, followed by the Bantu-speaking tribes who migrated from central Africa around 2,000 years ago.

In the late 19th century, the region was colonized by the British and was known as the British Protectorate of Bechuanaland. The British government took control to prevent the expansion of German and Boer territories. Despite this, the people of Botswana maintained a sense of identity and resisted exploitation.

Known for its large elephant population, Chobe National Park is a popular destination for wildlife enthusiasts. It offers spectacular boat safaris on the Chobe River and excellent game drives where visitors can spot lions, leopards, and numerous bird species. The park is also home to over 120,000 elephants, the largest concentration of elephants in Africa.

The Makgadikgadi Pans, remnants of a once vast lake, are another fascinating part of Botswana’s landscape. The salt pans cover a vast area and provide a unique, almost otherworldly experience. They are home to the San people and feature incredible wildlife migrations, including the zebra and wildebeest herds, and the chance to spot meerkats in their natural habitat.

Tsodilo Hills is a UNESCO World Heritage site known for its ancient rock art, believed to be over 20,000 years old. These sacred hills are a significant archaeological and cultural site, and they hold deep meaning for local communities. The site attracts both history buffs and spiritual seekers who want to explore one of Africa’s oldest and most important cultural landmarks.
